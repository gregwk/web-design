Web Design: Text and Background Colors

Contrast Ratios

WCAG 2.0 level AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text (see WebAIM.org). In the color blocks below, the light grey, the orange, and the violate all have a poor contrast ratio with white. Tomato and light sea green have a ratio that is close to 3, so they do not quite make the cut for the bold text. Dodger blue and gray have ratios above 3 but less than 4, so they are not acceptable for normal white text, but they are fine for large or bold text. Slate Blue is the only base color that has an acceptable ratio for normal white text.

To check the accessibility of text in Firefox, open the developer tools, chose "Accessibility" and then click the icon to the left (accessible object picker). Now when you hover over some text, you will see the contrast ratio of the text with the background.

Bad Color Combinations

These color combinations are sometimes used on websites to grab the reader's attention, but they are notoriously difficult to read.

Yellow on Green

Yellow on Green

Hues that are right next to each other on the color wheel are typically difficult to read when one is used as text and the other as a background. Yellow and green can be tempting to use together because they both grab your attention, especially if bright green is used, but this combination is difficult to read and should be avoided.

Green on Red

Green on Red

There is a saying in the fashion industry that goes: "Red and green should never be seen without a color in between." I'm not sure about fashion, but it's a good rule of thumb for websites. Any time your site has both red and green colors on it, you need to consider how it would look to someone with red-green color blindness, which is the most common form. You should never put green text on a red background or vice versa. This is hard on the eyes even for someone without red-green color blindness.

Purple on Green

Purple on Green

Purple and green is a popular color combination, but it shares many of the problems that red and green have, so be careful when using this combination on your website. Do not use purple text on a green background.

Red on Black

Red on Black

Many students love this combination for websites, and it's not surprising. The combination invokes intensity, drama, danger, and mystery. The colors are very popular in advertising. But red text on a black background is extremely difficult to read, and for those with red-green colorblindness, it can be nearly impossible. Try this experiment: Go to the accessibility tool in Firefox and simulate achromatopsia. This will effectively eliminate all color on this web page. Now look at the red text on the black background, you can barely see it. The contrast ratio is less than 2.5.

Keep in mind that, for all of the combinations listed here, it does not mean you must avoid using both colors on your site at the same time, just take care when using them next to each other, and do not use one for text when you are using the other for a background. If you want to see a site that handles red and black well, take a look at DVD.com. The logo is red, so they want to keep red as the accent color, and both the header and footer on most pages are black, but they are able to avoid red text on a black background.

Pure Black Backgrounds

Some web designers think that you should avoid pure black backgrounds in general, because no color can compete with black's intensity. This is probably a good idea. Some people recommend using a color close to black, but not pure black. An example would be the header from Amazon's website. At first glance it looks black, but if you examine it closely its actually a very dark shade of blue-green. The idea is that it's easier on the eyes, and the logo, especially the yellow arrow, does not look as jarring as it would on a pure black background.

Also, when you have a black or near-black background, consider making the text less intense also. For example, try using off-white instead of white, or lemon-yellow instead of yellow. Take a look at your favorite text editor in dark mode. Chances are that you will find that the background is not pure black and the text is not pure white.

Blue on Red

Blue on Red

Blue text on a red background is notoriously hard to read because it causes a "stereo effect" on our eyes. When we see blue text on a red background, our brains are trying to translate the 2D image into a 3D image, making the text seem to pop out and vibrate. This effeect also occurs with green text on red backgrounds. The effect is known as chromostereopsis, and needless to say, it plays havoc with readability.

Other Combinations to Avoid

Some sites that discuss color blindness also suggest avoiding these other color combinations:

  • Green & Red
  • Green & Brown
  • Blue & Purple
  • Green & Blue
  • Light Green & Yellow
  • Blue & Grey
  • Green & Grey
  • Green & Black

Along with these combinations and those we've already mentioned above, I would add a few more general things to consider when thinking about text and backgrounds.

  • Avoid putting text directly on a busy background, such as an image. If you want to put text over an image, which is a common thing to do, put it on its own colored rectangle or use a semi-transparent overlay.
  • Do not use intense (highly saturated) colors for both your text and background. You should probably reserve intense colors for accent colors, such as the background colors of your primary buttons and possibly your secondary buttons.
  • For your default text color, consider sticking with near black or near white.

Visual Accessibility Guidelines

We have talked a lot about what not to do regarding text and background colors, but what should we do? Here are some visual accessibility guidelines you should follow.

  • Always Use “alt” Tags – Using alt tags for all of your images is a simple web development guideline that you should always follow for visual accessibility. Also, images can be broken.
  • Use True Text; Not Text in Graphics – To make text more legible when enlarged, use true text as much as possible, rather than text in graphics. (WebAIM)
  • Maximize Contrast – To the extent possible, maximize the contrast of your web pages, including graphics, fonts, and backgrounds. (WebAIM)
  • Do Not Rely on Color – Ensure that color is not used as the only means of indicating or differentiating meaning or content. Color should never be used as the only means of conveying important information! (WebAIM)

This last bullet is particularly important, so lets look at a couple examples.

In the first example, both the success and failure alerts include icons that are associated with success and failure. They do not rely solely on the green and red colors to convey that information.

Your shopping cart has been successfully updated.
There was a problem trying to update your shopping cart.

In the next example, the error on form input is highlighted with a warning symbol rather than just a red outline of the input field.

Please enter your name.

Using Firefox for Help

A straightfoward approach to makeing sure your website is fairly visually accessible, particularly to color blind people, is to use the Firefox developer tools.

  • Choose the Accessibility tool inside the Firefox developer tools.
  • View your page using the achromatopsia simulation. Now check the following:
    • Can you still see all the information you want to see?
    • Can you distinguish between primary and secondary buttons?
    • Does the text still have a good contrast ratio with the background?

    If the answer to all of those questions is yes, your site should be reasonably accessible to colorblind users.

Example: Red text in Logo

In one of my courses, a student wanted to create a text logo involving red on a black background. The text logo looked something like this.

Not only does this font involve red text on a black background, but the text is small and thin. In fact, the font size of the logo text was actually smaller than it was for the default text of the page. Logo text should be larger and bolder, and you should use a font that looks good when it is large and bold. The student did not want to give up the red color altogether because he had already commited to it elsewhere on the site. We mitigated the readability problem by doing using some of the following "easy wins":

  • Get rid of pure black background
  • Don't make text color pure red
  • Minimize use of red font color
  • Increase size and weight of font
  • Use a distinctive font (Nunito)
  • Decrease line height

Nunito was a font that looked good at the larger font weight. Decreasing the line height is standard practice with larger fonts. We also left-justified the text and added a space before the symbol on the second line. This pushed the word books over so that the bottom of the "g" on the first line was above the "oo" in the word Books. We've still got the problem of a low contrast when colors are removed, but keeping red in the logo text was a self-imposed requirement.
