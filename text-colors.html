Web Design: Text and Background Colors

Contrast Ratios

WCAG 2.0 level AA requires a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for normal text and 3:1 for large text (see WebAIM.org). In the color blocks below, the light grey, the orange, and the violate all have a poor contrast ratio with white. Tomato and light sea green have a ratio that is close to 3, so they do not quite make the cut for the bold text. Dodger blue and gray have ratios above 3 but less than 4, so they are not acceptable for normal white text, but they are fine for large or bold text. Slate Blue is the only base color that has an acceptable ratio for normal white text.

To check the accessibility of text in Firefox, open the developer tools, chose "Accessibility" and then click the icon to the left (accessible object picker). Now when you hover over some text, you will see the contrast ratio of the text with the background.

Bad Color Combinations

These color combinations are sometimes used on websites to grab the reader's attention, but they are notoriously difficult to read.

Yellow on Green

Yellow on Green

Hues that are right next to each other on the color wheel are typically difficult to read when one is used as text and the other as a background. Yellow and green can be tempting to use together because they both grab your attention (especially if bright green is used). Both this combination is quite difficult to read and should be avoided as a rule.

Green on Red

Green on Red

Green and red side-by-side are very hard on the eyes. I lightened up the green significantly here but it is still difficult to read. More importantly, red-green colorblindness is the most common type of colorblindness. So you should always avoid green text on a red background (and vice-versa), but you should also be careful any time you are considering using both colors side-by-side on your website. Try to keep them separated with another, preferably neutral, color.

Purple on Green

Purple on Green

Purple and green is a popular color combination, but it shares many of the problems that red and green have, so be careful when using this combination on your website, and do not use purple text on a green background.

Red on Black

Red on Black

Many students love this combination for websites, and it's not surprising â€“ the combination invokes intensity and the occult. But red text on a black background is extremely difficult to read, and for those with red-green colorblindness, it's nearly impossible. Try this experiment: Go to the accessibility tool in Firefox and simulate achromatopsia. This will effectively eliminate all color on this web page. Now look at the red text on the black background, you can barely see it. The contrast ratio is less than 2.5.

If you want to see a site that handles red and black well, take a look at DVD.com. They're logo is red, so they want to keep red as the accent color, and they header and footer on most of the pages is black, but they avoid ever using red text on a black background.

Pure Black Backgrounds

Some web designers think that you should avoid pure black backgrounds in general, because no color can compete with black's intensity. Some of these people recommend using something close to black, but not black. An example would be the header from Amazon's website. At first glance it looks black, but if you examine it closely its actually a very dark shade of blue-green. The idea is that it's easier on the eyes, and the logo, especially the yellow arrow, does not look as jarring as it would on a pure black background.

Blue on Red

Blue on Red

Blue text on a red background is notoriously hard to read, and it has something to do with the way our eyes work. When we see blue text on a red background, our brains are perceiving (or trying to percieve) a 3D image from the 2D image in front of them. This also occurs with green text on red backgrounds. The effect is know as chromostereopsis. Needless to say, this effect plays havoc with readability.
